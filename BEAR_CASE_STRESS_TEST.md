## 1. AI Efficiency Scenario: The DeepSeek Threat

### The Bear Case
What if AI compute efficiency gains outpace demand growth, reducing datacenter power requirements by 30-50%?

### Data

**Inference cost is collapsing at unprecedented rates:**
- GPT-4 equivalent performance cost: $36/million tokens (March 2023) to $0.40/million tokens (2025) -- a roughly 99% decline in two years
- Median inference cost decline: 200x per year post-January 2024, accelerating from 50x/year pre-2024
- DeepSeek R1 achieved a 45:1 efficiency gain over GPT-4 equivalent while training on just 2,048 H800 GPUs versus the 16,000+ chips used by competitors
- Stanford research documents 5.3x improvement in local AI intelligence-per-watt from 2023 to 2025 (3.1x from model improvements, 1.7x from hardware)
- DeepSeek's servers consume an estimated 50-75% less energy than Nvidia's latest GPU units

**The counter-argument (Jevons Paradox):**
- The bull rebuttal is that efficiency drives adoption, which drives total consumption higher
- DeepSeek R1's 45:1 efficiency gain "could triple AI's inference workloads by 2028"
- MIT Technology Review notes DeepSeek's training savings are "offset by its more intensive techniques for answering questions, and by the long answers they produce"

### Stress Test for Portfolio
If efficiency gains reduce projected datacenter power demand by 30-50%:
- **CEG, VST:** Nuclear PPA premiums compress; 20-year contracts may price in demand that doesn't materialize at expected scale
- **GEV:** 83 GW gas turbine backlog (sold out through 2029) becomes vulnerable to cancellations or deferrals
- **ETN:** Nine-year datacenter construction backlog could see project delays or scope reductions
- **OKLO:** Pre-revenue SMR play with $0 revenue through 2025 and no commercial timeline until 2027-2028 at earliest becomes even more speculative

### Probability Assessment
**Moderate-High (35-45%).** Efficiency is improving faster than almost anyone predicted. However, the Jevons Paradox has strong historical support -- cheaper compute historically expands the addressable market. The bear case requires efficiency gains to overwhelm demand growth, which has rarely happened in computing history. The more realistic risk is a 12-24 month demand delay, not demand destruction.

## 2. Demand Overestimation: The Forecasting Track Record

### The Bear Case
Infrastructure demand forecasts have a terrible historical track record. Are datacenter power projections the new fiber optic buildout?

### Data

**Current forecast divergence is enormous:**
- Goldman Sachs: Datacenter power demand surges 165-175% by 2030 vs. 2023; CAGR of 17% (2025-2028), from ~55 GW to 92 GW by 2027
- Goldman bull case: 20% CAGR; Goldman bear case: 14% CAGR
- IEA: Global datacenter electricity consumption up ~15% between 2024 and 2030 (far more conservative)
- McKinsey: 3.5x increase in datacenter capacity demand (2025-2030)
- S&P Global: Grid-power demand up 22% in 2025, nearly triple by 2030
- The spread between the most bullish (Goldman) and most conservative (IEA) estimates is roughly 10x

**Forecasts are already being revised down:**
- EIA cut its 2026 electricity generation growth forecast by 1.3%
- ERCOT growth projections shrunk from 15.7% to 9.6% for 2026
- American Electric Power Ohio cut its datacenter pipeline in half, from 30+ GW to 13 GW (after imposing a tariff requiring upfront payment commitments)
- Between 2010 and 2018, global datacenter electricity use was basically flat despite massive growth in cloud adoption, as efficiency gains absorbed demand

**Hyperscaler capex may not translate to proportional power demand:**
- Combined hyperscaler CapEx: ~$200B (2024), ~$350B (2025), projected >$600B (2026)
- Much of this spending goes to GPUs, networking, and software -- not facilities
- GPUs are getting more power-efficient each generation even as total spend rises

### Historical Precedents for Overestimation
1. **Fiber Optic Bubble (1999-2001):** $500 billion invested; by 2002, only 2.7% of fiber was being used; by mid-2000s, 85% remained dark; bandwidth costs fell 90%
2. **5G Tower Projections:** Early forecasts of massive small-cell densification have not materialized at projected rates; coverage gaps persist but build-out pace was significantly overstated
3. **Cloud Computing Power (2010-2018):** Predictions of datacenter electricity explosions proved wrong as efficiency improvements (virtualization, PUE improvements) kept consumption flat

### Stress Test for Portfolio
If datacenter power demand follows the IEA's conservative 15% growth path rather than Goldman's 175%:
- **Every name in the portfolio** is affected, but particularly CEG, VST, GEV, ETN, and OKLO, which are priced for the high-demand scenario
- Nuclear PPAs and gas turbine backlogs may represent stranded demand commitments
- Midstream (KMI) and LNG exports (LNG) have diversified demand drivers beyond datacenters, offering some insulation

### Probability Assessment
**Moderate (25-35%).** The range of credible forecasts is so wide that even the "base case" could prove to be the "bull case." The fact that AEP Ohio saw its pipeline drop by half when requiring firm financial commitments is a warning sign that not all announced demand is firm.

## 3. PPA Renegotiation / Cancellation Risk

### The Bear Case
What if tech companies renegotiate or exit long-term power purchase agreements as economics shift?

### Data

**Current PPA landscape:**
- Microsoft: 20-year PPA with Constellation Energy (Three Mile Island Unit 1 restart, targeting 2028)
- Meta: 20-year PPA with Constellation for 1.1 GW from Clinton Clean Energy Center; separate deals with TerraPower, Oklo, and Vistra totaling up to 6.6 GW
- Amazon: Sought 480 MW co-location at Susquehanna (Talen Energy) but FERC rejected the expansion

**The FERC Rejection Precedent:**
- On November 1, 2024, FERC voted 2-1 to reject the Amazon-Talen Susquehanna ISA amendment (expanding from 300 MW to 480 MW)
- FERC concluded PJM "failed to meet the high burden" of justifying the deviation from standard interconnection rules
- This forced Talen to restructure the deal as a 1,920 MW "front of the meter" PPA that doesn't require FERC approval
- On April 16, 2025, FERC denied the Talen-Amazon agreement again on rehearing
- The December 2025 FERC order created new co-location rules, but the regulatory framework remains in flux

**NuScale UAMPS Cancellation -- A Cautionary Tale:**
- November 8, 2023: NuScale and UAMPS terminated the 462 MW Carbon Free Power Project in Idaho
- Costs had escalated from $5.3B to $9.3B (75% increase); target power price rose from $58/MWh to $89/MWh (53% increase)
- Subscription fell short of the 80% threshold needed to proceed
- NuScale stock dropped 32.9% in one day to $2.08
- The $1.4B DOE cost-share award was insufficient to prevent cancellation

**Legal protections for PPAs are strong but not absolute:**
- Most large PPAs include force majeure, regulatory change, and material adverse change clauses
- If regulatory frameworks change (e.g., co-location rules tightened), termination clauses may be triggered
- If delivered power costs significantly exceed market alternatives, economic pressure to renegotiate intensifies

### Stress Test for Portfolio
- **CEG:** Directly exposed via the Three Mile Island restart PPA and Clinton PPA with Meta; any renegotiation or delay destroys the premium valuation (current P/E: ~30x, 65% above utility sector average)
- **VST:** Meta PPA exposure; current P/E of ~55-61x (184% above sector average) provides no margin for error
- **OKLO:** Meta agreement is for future SMR capacity that does not yet exist; cancellation risk compounds execution risk

### Probability Assessment
**Low-Moderate (15-25%).** Tech companies have strong incentives to maintain PPAs given power scarcity and their own net-zero commitments. However, the NuScale precedent shows that projects can be cancelled when economics deteriorate, and FERC regulatory uncertainty adds a meaningful overlay of risk.

## 4. Natural Gas Price Collapse: The $1.50-2.00 Scenario

### The Bear Case
What if Henry Hub returns to the historic lows of 2024 and stays there?

### Data

**We already saw $1.50-$2.00 gas in 2024:**
- 2024 annual average: $2.21/MMBtu -- the lowest inflation-adjusted annual average ever recorded
- Monthly low: $1.51/MMBtu (March 2024) -- all-time record low
- February, March, April, and August 2024 were the four lowest inflation-adjusted months ever recorded
- The 68% decline from 2022 to 2024 was the largest two-year drop on record
- 2023 annual average was $2.54/MMBtu

**Current forecasts suggest recovery, but forecasts have been wrong before:**
- EIA 2026 forecast: ~$4.27-4.30/MMBtu
- Morgan Stanley bull case: above $5/MMBtu in 2026
- New LNG export capacity (Plaquemines, Corpus Christi Stage 3, Golden Pass) expected to tighten domestic supply
- But: if global LNG demand disappoints or production growth overshoots, prices could revisit 2024 lows

**Breakeven analysis:**
- EQT corporate breakeven: ~$2.00/MMBtu for first dollar of free cash flow; $2.40-2.45/Mcf for full costs
- Appalachian (Marcellus) breakeven: $2.00-2.50/MMBtu
- Haynesville breakeven: $3.50/MMBtu for profitability; at $4/MMBtu only 13% of acreage is profitable
- At $2/MMBtu, virtually all Haynesville production is uneconomic

**Impact on portfolio names at $1.50-2.00 Henry Hub:**

| Company | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| **EQT** | FCF goes negative; $7B net debt becomes a problem; long-term projection of $10-25B cumulative FCF (at $2.75-5.00) is destroyed |
| **KMI** | Pipeline throughput volumes decline as producers shut in; lower tariff revenues on commodity-exposed contracts |
| **LNG** | Cheniere is relatively insulated due to take-or-pay contracts; low domestic gas actually improves margins on international arbitrage |
| **CEG, VST** | Gas-fired generation becomes cheaper, potentially reducing nuclear's competitiveness; mixed impact |

### Historical frequency of sustained sub-$2 gas:
- 2020: COVID-driven collapse, brief
- 2023-2024: Extended 18+ month period of sub-$3 gas
- 2024: Four months below $2.00 (monthly average basis)
- The probability of sustained sub-$2 gas is non-trivial given the structural oversupply risk from continued Marcellus/Utica productivity gains

### Probability Assessment
**Moderate (20-30%).** The 2024 experience proves that sub-$2 gas is not a tail event -- it happened less than two years ago. New LNG export capacity should provide a demand floor, but a global recession or production surge could push prices back to distressed levels. EQT's low breakeven provides some protection, but KMI's throughput volumes would suffer.

## 5. Nuclear Regulatory Reversal

### The Bear Case
What if the NRC tightens SMR licensing, FERC reverses co-location approval, or the regulatory path for new nuclear proves longer and more expensive than expected?

### Data

**Current NRC licensing timeline:**
- Part 53 (advanced reactor regulation) may not be ready for applications until 2026-2027; implementation rule expected in 2027
- Historically, nuclear licensing has taken "more than a decade" -- unattractive for hyperscalers needing power in 2-3 years
- TerraPower's Kemmerer Unit 1 construction permit: NRC final vote scheduled for February 2026; permit expected "sometime after mid-2026"
- NuScale US 460 Standard Design Approval issued May 2025 (462 MW) -- two months ahead of schedule, a rare bright spot
- NRC expects 25+ license applications for SMR and advanced reactors by 2029

**FERC Co-location Regulatory Whiplash:**
- November 2024: FERC rejects Amazon-Talen Susquehanna ISA expansion (2-1 vote)
- April 2025: FERC denies rehearing
- December 2025: FERC issues unanimous order creating new PJM co-location rules with three new transmission service options
- The 14-month journey from rejection to new rules demonstrates how unpredictable FERC policy is
- DOE has directed FERC to accelerate datacenter interconnection, but federal-state jurisdiction concerns remain unresolved

**NuScale/UAMPS: The SMR Cautionary Tale:**
- The only NRC-certified SMR design (NuScale US460) has zero operating projects
- Cost escalation from $5.3B to $9.3B killed the first commercial project
- Target power price of $89/MWh is not competitive with gas ($30-50/MWh) or solar+storage (~$40-60/MWh)

**Key regulatory risks for 2026-2027:**
- NRC could impose additional safety requirements post-certification that increase costs
- Environmental litigation (site-specific challenges) could delay individual projects
- FERC's new co-location rules could face legal challenges from utilities or ratepayer advocates
- State-level opposition to nuclear siting remains a factor in many jurisdictions

### Stress Test for Portfolio
- **OKLO:** Most exposed. Pre-revenue, $15.7B market cap, $0 revenue, burning ~$30M/quarter. NRC licensing delays could push commercial operations past 2030. At current burn rate, ~7.5 years of runway, but any delay past that window is existential
- **CCJ:** Uranium demand growth depends on new reactor construction. If SMR timelines slip by 3-5 years, near-term uranium demand growth slows. However, CCJ has 230M lbs under long-term contract and existing reactor fuel demand provides a floor
- **CEG:** Less directly exposed (operates existing plants), but nuclear co-location premium in its stock price could compress if regulatory environment turns hostile

### Probability Assessment
**Moderate (25-35%).** Nuclear regulatory environments are inherently slow-moving and unpredictable. The NuScale cancellation and FERC whiplash demonstrate that even "approved" projects face enormous execution risk. However, bipartisan political support for nuclear power and the Trump administration's pro-nuclear stance provide a favorable near-term backdrop.

## 7. Tariff Escalation: Real Cost Impacts

### The Bear Case
Current and proposed tariffs on steel, aluminum, copper, and solar panels are already increasing energy infrastructure project costs. How much worse can it get?

### Data

**Current tariff rates on key materials:**
- Steel: 25% tariff
- Aluminum: 25% tariff
- Copper (semi-finished and derivatives): 50% tariff (effective August 2025)
- Chinese solar panels (finished): 175% tariff
- Polysilicon, wafers, and cells: 195% tariff
- Southeast Asian solar imports: 46% (Vietnam), 49% (Cambodia)

**Measured cost impacts:**
- Copper wiring prices: up 18% since January 2025
- Steel conduit costs: up 14%
- Electrical panels: up 22% (combined steel and copper tariffs)
- Solar panels: 3-4 cents/watt increase; balance of system adds another 2-3 cents/watt
- Transformers: expected additional 20-30% price increase over coming years
- BCG estimates copper tariffs alone represent an $8.6B cost to the U.S. economy
- A US solar project is now 54% more expensive than the same plant in Europe, and 85% more expensive than one built in China

**Transformer shortage compounds tariff pain:**
- 30% supply deficit for power transformers in 2025
- 10% supply deficit for distribution transformers
- Lead times stretched to 2-4 years (vs. months in 2020)
- Electrical steel -- a specialty input -- is in short domestic supply
- 50% steel tariff applies to the steel content of even imported transformers

**Project cancellations already underway:**
- $7.7B in clean manufacturing projects cancelled in Q1 2025 alone (vs. $1.8B in all of 2024)
- Materials account for 20-30% of transmission project costs; tariffs could slow or reduce the scope of development

### Stress Test for Portfolio

| Company | Tariff Exposure | Impact |
|---------|----------------|--------|
| **NEE** | Very High | Largest renewable developer in US; solar/wind project costs increase 15-25%; project returns compress |
| **BEP** | High | Global renewable portfolio mitigates US-specific tariffs, but new US projects become more expensive |
| **GEV** | Moderate | "Local-for-local" manufacturing mitigates but doesn't eliminate tariff exposure; wind segment margins pressured |
| **ETN** | Moderate | Electrical equipment inputs (copper, steel) subject to 25-50% tariffs; management expects dollar-for-dollar pass-through but margin guidance lowered to 24-24.4% |
| **CEG** | Low | Existing nuclear fleet has minimal new-build exposure |
| **KMI** | Low-Moderate | Pipeline maintenance and expansion costs increase but existing infrastructure is in place |

### Probability Assessment
**High (60-70%).** This is not a hypothetical -- tariffs are already in effect and their costs are already being measured. The question is whether they escalate further (highly possible given the current political environment) or are eventually rolled back. Every new renewable or grid project in the US is now significantly more expensive than it was 18 months ago.

## 8. Historical Analogies: Cautionary Tales

### 8A. The Fiber Optic Bubble (1999-2001)

**What happened:**
- The Telecommunications Act of 1996 unleashed a race to build fiber networks
- Over $500 billion was invested in five years, mostly debt-financed
- Companies assumed exponential internet traffic growth would absorb all capacity
- By 2002, only 2.7% of installed fiber was being used
- By mid-2000s, 85% of fiber remained "dark" (unused)
- Bandwidth costs fell 90% as supply overwhelmed demand
- Dozens of telecom companies went bankrupt (WorldCom, Global Crossing, etc.)

**Parallels to current AI infrastructure buildout:**
- Exponential demand projections driving massive upfront investment
- Combined hyperscaler CapEx: $200B (2024) growing to >$600B (2026)
- Debt-financed infrastructure buildout across the value chain
- A shared assumption that demand will grow into supply
- Everyone building simultaneously, creating potential oversupply

**Key differences:**
- 1990s fiber was built by highly leveraged, often single-product telecom companies; today's buildout is funded by the most cash-rich companies in history (Apple, Microsoft, Google, Meta)
- Fiber had no immediate use case beyond bandwidth; AI infrastructure has measurable revenue generation today
- Today's datacenter PPAs are backed by long-term contracts, not speculative demand

**The uncomfortable truth:** The fiber eventually found its use -- it became the backbone of modern cloud computing, streaming, and mobile internet. But the timing was off by a decade. The investors who built the fiber were destroyed even though the thesis was ultimately correct.

### 8B. The Shale Oil/Gas Boom-Bust (2014-2016)

**What happened:**
- Horizontal drilling and hydraulic fracturing unlocked massive US oil and gas reserves
- Oil prices above $90/barrel (2011-2014) made even marginal shale wells profitable
- The industry levered up on cheap debt: ~$250 billion in loans by 2014
- Overproduction crashed WTI from $106/barrel (June 2014) to $32/barrel (January 2016) -- a 70% decline
- 100+ companies filed for bankruptcy (2015-2016) with $82 billion in debt listed in filings
- Over $300 billion in cumulative losses since 2010
- 67,000 employees in 2014 fell to 26,000
- The industry had never been consistently free-cash-flow positive at the aggregate level

**Parallels to current energy infrastructure buildout:**
- Low-cost capital funding rapid capacity expansion
- Assumption that demand growth would absorb all new supply
- Producers competing to build faster, creating a supply glut
- Many individual companies not generating free cash flow despite headline growth

**Key differences:**
- Shale was a commodity business with zero demand visibility; datacenter power demand has contracted long-term offtake
- Energy infrastructure companies today are generally better capitalized and more disciplined than 2014-era shale operators
- The energy infrastructure thesis is about serving known, contracted customers, not producing into a spot market

### What Both Analogies Teach Us

1. **Timing risk is the real killer.** The thesis can be right and the investment can still lose. Fiber optic investors were correct about internet traffic growth -- just a decade early. Shale investors were correct about producibility -- but oversupply destroyed pricing.

2. **Debt amplifies pain.** Both busts were worsened by excessive leverage. The portfolio names with the most debt (NEE at ~$90B, BEP at ~$36B) face the same amplification risk.

3. **Consensus forecasts cluster around optimistic scenarios.** When everyone agrees on a bullish demand outlook, the marginal dollar of investment gets deployed, eventually creating overcapacity.

4. **The winners are not the builders -- they are the users.** The companies that profited most from fiber were Google, Amazon, and Netflix -- not the telecom companies that laid it. The companies that profited most from shale were chemical companies and LNG exporters who got cheap feedstock -- not the producers.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Valuation is the most immediate risk.** CEG (30x PE) and VST (55-61x PE) are trading at unprecedented premiums for utility stocks. These multiples assume the bull thesis plays out perfectly. Any deviation -- even a modest demand shortfall or timing delay -- creates significant downside from multiple compression alone.

2. **Tariffs are not hypothetical -- they are real and measurable today.** Unlike most other scenarios in this document, tariff escalation is already happening. Copper +18%, steel conduit +14%, electrical panels +22%, transformers +20-30%. Every new energy infrastructure project in the US is more expensive than 18 months ago.

3. **The NuScale precedent demands respect.** A government-backed, NRC-certified SMR project saw costs nearly double and was cancelled. This is the single most relevant data point for evaluating OKLO.

4. **Efficiency gains are accelerating, not slowing.** 200x annual decline in inference costs (post-January 2024) means the demand models underlying current valuations could be obsolete within 12 months. The Jevons Paradox may save the thesis in aggregate, but it won't save every individual capital project.

5. **The strongest names are those with contracted, diversified revenue.** LNG (take-or-pay), KMI (fee-based midstream), and CCJ (long-term uranium contracts) have the most durable business models under bear scenarios. The weakest are those most dependent on future demand materialization (OKLO, CEG at current valuations, VST at current valuations).

6. **Debt is the amplifier.** NEE ($90B) and BEP ($36B) face the greatest risk of value destruction in a rising-rate or demand-disappointment scenario, echoing the leverage-driven collapses of both the fiber optic and shale busts.
